Social media has become a significant influence on the everyday experiences of students. Social apps such as TikTok, Instagram, and Snapchat are not merely entertaining platforms; they represent environments where students spend many hours every day. Although such platforms provide opportunities for communication and creativity, they also consume educational time.

The role of social media in education remains the point of discussion. Certain scholars imply that it is possible to make social media into a learning tool in case it is implemented properly. Even some teachers have found success creating TikTok accounts where they share learning tips and tricks.
I can teach more kids about photosynthesis in a 30-second video than I could reach in my classroom all week.
This perspective shows how social media can actually expand educational reach when used strategically. However, this optimistic view overlooks the reality of how most students actually interact with these platforms daily.

The American Psychological Association has associated too much reliance on social media with poor sleep, having a short attention span, and even a decline in performance. In a report conducted in 2022, they found that students who used more than three hours per day on social media were 40% more likely to report lower grades and showed measurable increases in stress hormones.
95% of teens report having access to a smartphone, and 45% say they use the internet 'almost constantly.'
This constant connectivity creates what researchers call "continuous partial attention," where students never fully focus on academic tasks.

In my opinion, social media is damaging students, particularly where there is no restriction on the use of screen time or content. The applications are made to be addictive. The perpetual scroll, dopamine rushes upon liking posts, and the non-stop notifications do not leave students unattached.
The short-term, dopamine-driven feedback loops that we have created are destroying how society works.
This confession from someone who helped build these platforms proves that the addictive design is intentional, not accidental.

I don't want to say that social media is bad altogether. However, it is causing more damage than good without proper education about healthy consumption and positive online behavior. Schools and parents need to work together to teach students how to use these tools without being controlled by them, because the current situation is clearly not working for most students' academic success.
